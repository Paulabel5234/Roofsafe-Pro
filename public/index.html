Cal/OSHA Title 8 · §1509(e)

This isn’t optional. It’s the law.

California’s Title 8 Construction Safety Orders spell out exactly what roofing contractors are required to do — and how often.

Supervisory employees shall conduct ‘toolbox’ or ‘tailgate’ safety meetings, or equivalent, with their crews at least every 10 working days to emphasize safety.

8 CCR §1509(e) · California Code of Regulations, Title 8 · Construction Safety Orders

What this means for roofers

  • Cal/OSHA inspectors use this language as the basis for citing contractors who fail to hold regular crew safety meetings.
  • Fines for violations can reach $25,000 per incident, with repeat violations carrying higher penalties.
  • Every 10 working days is the minimum. The bundle hands you 30 roofing-specific talks — enough for over a year of meetings on that cadence.
  • If a new hazard appears — extreme heat, a near miss, a new work phase — hold an extra talk regardless of schedule.

4 regulatory requirements your IIPP probably doesn’t cover.

Most contractors downloaded a template years ago and filed it away. The problem? The rules kept moving. Here are four requirements — some old, some new — that pre-2017 templates miss entirely.

Effective June 23, 2017 Significant Cal/OSHA §1532.3

Construction silica standard — covers roofing demo and concrete-tile work

Most IIPPs in circulation predate this rule. Roofing work that involves cutting concrete tile, slate, or otherwise disturbing silica-containing materials falls under §1532.3. A checklist line that says “wear a dust mask” does not satisfy the exposure-assessment, engineering-control, and respiratory-protection requirements.

Cal/OSHA serious citations up to ~$25,000; willful exposures significantly higher.
July 23, 2024 Critical Cal/OSHA §3396

Brand-new Indoor Heat Illness Standard — a rule that simply didn’t exist before

An entirely new regulation covering attics, enclosed spaces, and interior reroof areas at 82°F+. If your IIPP was written before July 23, 2024, this section is completely absent — because the law didn’t exist yet.

Cal/OSHA general up to ~$16,000 · Serious up to ~$25,000 · Willful up to ~$165,000.
January 13, 2025 Significant 29 CFR 1926.95(c) — Federal OSHA PPE Fit Rule

“Good enough” PPE is no longer legal — every worker needs properly fitted gear

The days of one harness in the truck shared by the crew are over. If a worker wears an oversized harness or ill-fitting hard hat, that’s now a citable OSHA violation — even if the PPE is technically present.

PPE-related citations automatically enhanced if fit is not documented.
July 1, 2025 Critical Cal/OSHA §1731

The 6-foot rule is now law — California’s biggest fall-protection change in decades

Fall protection is now required at 6 feet for residential-type roofing on slopes from 0:12 to 7:12 — down from 15 feet. Any pitch steeper than 7:12 requires fall protection regardless of height. Any IIPP written before July 2025 cites a standard that no longer exists; if a Cal/OSHA inspector sees “15 feet” in your plan, that’s an immediate citation.

Cal/OSHA serious up to ~$25,000 · Willful (citing outdated standard) up to ~$165,000.

Penalty maximums are approximate — Cal/OSHA and federal OSHA adjust both schedules every January. Citations are summarized for readability; consult the underlying regulation text or a qualified safety professional before relying on any specific provision for compliance.
